This directory demonstrates the calculation of the electric field distribution inside the coating layers. A narrow band pass filter and a quarter wave mirror are used as examples, since in both cases electric field distribution has some features that are worth mentioning.

In the File -> Project menu of OptiLayer, open the directory LEC24D12. The substrate Glass n=1.52 and the layer materials n=2.35 and n=1.46 are loaded into RAM. There are two files in the target database, Thelen’s prototype for filter with 4 mirrors and 3 spacers and QWM-19 &600 nm. Load the first of these files into RAM. Open the T&R&Phase evaluation window and the Refractive Index Profile window (Thickness tab) to see the transmittance and layer optical thicknesses of this prototype.

Open the Analysis->Field option to see the distribution of the electric field within the coating layers at the wavelength of 600 nm.  Note that the window actually shows the square of the electric field modulus, that is, the electric field intensity. The intensity is presented as a percentage relative to the intensity of the incident light in the incident medium. The highest intensity values are observed in spacer layers and nearby mirror layers. This is a direct consequence of the resonant structure of the narrow band pass filter under consideration: the spacer layers form resonance cavities inside the filter.

Load the second file QWM-19 &600 nm into RAM. The evaluation window shows the transmittance of this 19-layer mirror with a central wavelength of 600 nm.

In the Analysis->Field window, look at the distribution of the electric field inside the mirror layers at a central wavelength of 600 nm.\

 Note that in this case the highest intensity value is observed at the boundary of the two outer layers. The intensity decreases rapidly as one moves from the outer layers to layers located at a greater distance from the outer boundary of the mirror.


 Note on the maximum value of the electric field intensity inside the mirror layers. It turns out that by varying the thicknesses of mirror layers, it is impossible to reduce the maximum intensity below a certain value. This value is estimated as 4/(n_H)^2 of the intensity of the incident light. Here n_H is the refractive index of the high-index layers. In our case, n_H=2.35 and we find that the maximum intensity cannot be lower than 72.4% of the intensity of the incident light. In the Field window we see that this exactly what we have at the boundary of the two outer layers. The discussed result is important for the high-power applications of optical coatings. By varying the thicknesses of the mirror layers, one can only shift the positions of the electric field maximum from the boundaries of the layers, but not reduce the magnitude of this maximum.

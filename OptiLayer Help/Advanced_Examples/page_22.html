In amorphous states of thin film materials, variations in their refractive indices depend on variations in the densities of these states. The latter depend on various parameters of the deposition process, such as the energy and flux density of the deposited atoms,  the flux of oxygen atoms, if reactive deposition is used, the flux of assisting atoms, if there is additional ion assistance. All these parameters fluctuate and this causes fluctuations in the film packing density and in the refractive index of the growing layer. This is called the bulk inhomogeneity of coating layers. In layers deposited by modern deposition processes, the bulk inhomogeneity is small and there are only rare situations where it should be taken into account.  Nevertheless, it is useful to become familiar with the effects caused by bulk inhomogeneity, and such an acquaintance is the main purpose of this example.

In the File -> Project menu of OptiLayer, open the directory LEC24D18. The datafiles needed to run this exercise are already loaded into RAM. These are the substrate Glass n=1.52, the layer materials n=2.35 and n=1.46, and the design QWM-19, c.w. 600 nm. Open the T&R&Phase option in the Analysis menu. Use the Axis option to display the transmittance of the 19-layer QWM in the spectral range from 150 nm to 800 nm.

The measure of the bulk inhomogeneity is the degree of inhomogeneity, the absolute value of which is defined as the difference in the layer refractive indices at its right and left boundaries, divided by the mean refractive index of the layer. Most often, the degree of inhomogeneity is expressed as a percentage, i.e. as its absolute value multiplied by 100%. Both negative and positive values of the degree of inhomogeneity in the range of several percent are possible. Positive values up to 2-3%, and sometimes higher, are observed in the case of high-energy deposition processes. Negative values are typical for low-energy deposition processes.

In the Analysis menu, select the Inhomogeneity option. At the Characteristic tab of the Inhomogeneity Setup window, select the transmittance (T) at zero incidence angle (normal incidence) to be displayed.

Use the Inhomogeneities tab to enter the degrees of inhomogeneity for the high and low index layers. Let us start with -3% degrees of inhomogeneity for all mirror layers.

Click OK to start the analysis procedure. The upper part of the Inhomogeneity Analysis window displays the transmittance of an ideal mirror without inhomogeneity (black curve) and the transmittance of a mirror with inhomogeneous layers (red curve). The difference between the black and red curves is barely noticeable at all wavelength, except for a small vicinity of 300 nm. The refractive index profiles of the ideal mirror and the mirror with inhomogeneous layers are shown in the lower part of the window.

Use the Parameters tab in the lower left corner of the window to specify other degrees of inhomogeneity (e.g. +3% for high index layers and 0% for low index layers). Note that you can easily set different values for odd and even layers using the Apply to: field in the Column editor window.

Computational experiments with the degrees of inhomogeneity in the range of a few percent lead to the same conclusion: the effect of bulk inhomogeneity is noticeable only near 300 nm.

 Note that the wavelength of 300 nm is half the mirror central wavelength. It corresponds to a frequency twice the frequency corresponding to the mirror central wavelength and is important for coatings used in some laser systems. Here it may be necessary to take into account bulk inhomogeneity in coating layers.

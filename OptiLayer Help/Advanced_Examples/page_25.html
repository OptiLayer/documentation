 Note: Due to the continuous improvement of OptiLayer options and random nature of some of their elements, the results obtained may differ from those described below.

The main purpose of this example is to demonstrate the Broadband Monitoring Simulation option of the Monitoring menu, which is used to simulate the production of optical coatings with broadband monitoring. In addition, the existence of a strong error self-compensation effect associated with broadband monitoring is discussed.

In the File -> Project menu of OptiLayer, open the directory LEC24D21. Datafiles needed to run this exercise are already loaded into RAM. These are the substrate BK7, the layer materials SiO2 and ZrO2, and the design 28-layer polarizer for 1064 nm. The design is a Brewster angle polarizer for operation at an incidence angle of 55.6 degrees. Open the T&R&Phase window and select Ts and Tp transmittance plots at 55.6-degree incidence in the spectral range of 950-1150 nm to view its spectral characteristics.

Compared to the error analysis option provided by the Pre-production Estimation of Errors menu item, the considered simulation option provides a more in-depth analysis of production errors caused by various error factors associated with the deposition process and the monitoring procedure. The Broadband Monitoring Simulation option is organized as a six-step dialog.

In the first step of the dialog, set the deposition rates of the high and low index materials to 0.3 nm/s and 0.5 nm/s with random fluctuations of these values having rms levels of 0.05 nm/s and 0.1 nm/s, respectively. For both materials, set the correlation time to 3 s (Corr. time field). This value determines the average intervals between rate fluctuations over time.

Click the Next button to proceed to the second step of the dialog. This step allows you to set parameters related to refractive index inaccuracies (upper part of the setup window) and inaccuracies in closing the shutters used to terminate layer depositions (shutter delay fields in the bottom part of the window). Let us leave zeros in the refractive index error fields. Shutter delays are generated as normally distributed errors with a given mean and standard deviation. Set the mean to 1 s and the standard deviation to 0.5 s (Shutter delay RMS field). In the experiments involving depositions of ZrO2 layers, it was noted that there may be additional inaccuracies in the thickness of the first ZrO2 layer from the substrate. To simulate these inaccuracies, we will exclude the first layer from the optical monitoring procedure and introduce random errors directly into its thickness. To do this, check the Exclude checkbox in the first layer line in the middle part of the window and set the error level in the Relative Thickness Error field. Enter 3% in this field. The error will be simulated by a normal distribution with a zero mean and a standard deviation equal to 3% of the first layer thickness. The Exclude option can also be used to simulate the production of optical coatings, where some layers are monitored by a deposition time rather than optically. This type of monitoring is most often used in the case of designs with thin layers.

In the third step of the dialog, the monitoring characteristic and the parameters of the monitoring procedure are set. We select normal incidence transmittance with an interval between broadband data scans of 3 seconds (Scan interval field). Let us set the spectral region of monitoring from 658 nm to 1172 nm with 509 spectral points. By entering the layer numbers in the Preview Layer field, you can select the theoretical transmittance at the end of layer deposition for viewing in the right part of the window.

Move on to the fourth step of the dialog. At this step, the parameters of measurement data errors are set. We introduce random errors of 1% into the transmittance data, which are simulated at each wavelength by independent normal distributions with zero means and the same standard deviations of 1%. Further, we also introduce fluctuations of the transmittance as a whole (parameters in the Fluctuations fields). These fluctuations are modeled as normally distributed values that can occur in the specified time intervals. We set the level of fluctuations to 0.5% (Drift field) and mean values of time intervals (Mean time field) and their standard deviations (RMS field) to 5 s and 1 s, respectively. The simulated transmittance at the end of layer deposition can be viewed in the right part of the window.

Let us proceed to the fifth step of the dialog. Since the computational experiments are going much faster than prescribed by the deposition rates, we can apply an acceleration factor to the computational deposition process. This is done using the Time multiplier rate field. Clicking the Start button, starts the simulation experiment.

Its progress is reflected in the right part of the window in terms of spectral characteristics. Here Theoretical denotes the theoretical transmittance at the end of layer deposition, Actual denotes the “actually” measured transmittance of the structure created by the deposition part of the simulation software, and Estimated shows the transmittance corresponding to the design structure calculated by the algorithm used to analyze the broadband measurement data. The corresponding layer thicknesses T, A, E are shown in the left part of the window. The theoretical layer thicknesses T do not change during layer deposition, while the actual and estimated thicknesses A and E grow. The deposition of each layer is terminated when the estimated thickness E reaches the theoretically planned thickness T.

After the simulation experiment is completed, we can move on to the final sixth step of the dialog. At this step, the results of the performed experiment can be examined from different points of view. Using the Spectral Performance tab at the bottom of the window, it is possible to compare the transmittance of the “actually” deposited coating with the transmittance of the theoretical design.

Errors in layer thicknesses can be viewed in a graphical form (Relative errors and Absolute errors tabs)

and in a tabular form (Thickness tab).

If errors in refractive indices were simulated, then it is possible to view them using the Refractive indices tab.

Since optical monitoring is performed under normal light incidence, we do not see the Ts and Tp characteristics in the last step of the simulation dialog. To see them in the T&R&Phase evaluation window, click the Load button on the right side of the 6-th step window. It can be seen that despite the noticeable errors in the thicknesses of many layers, both characteristics are excellent. This is a direct consequence of the error self-compensation effect associated with broadband optical monitoring.

To understand the essence of the error self-compensation effect, it is necessary to compare the changes in the Ts and Tp characteristics caused by correlated errors in layer thicknesses and uncorrelated random thickness errors. Let us do this for the Tp characteristic. In the T&R&Phase window, change the T-scale to 95 – 100%. The design database contains three designs cm 1, cm 2, cm 3 with correlated thickness errors obtained during simulation experiments with broadband monitoring. Load these designs one by one to make sure that the changes in the Tp are insignificant.

After that, select the Error Analysis… option in the Analysis menu, which allows you to estimate the effect of uncorrelated errors in layer thicknesses. Select the Tp characteristic at 55.6-degree incidence and set The number of tests to 20 with a 1 sec Pause after each plot.

In the Thickness tab, set the rms level of absolute thickness errors to 2 nm. This is approximately the rms level of correlated errors in the BBM simulation experiments above. Set the level of relative errors to zero. Click Run to start the error analysis. It is clearly seen that the impact of uncorrelated errors is much worse than the impact of correlated errors discussed earlier.

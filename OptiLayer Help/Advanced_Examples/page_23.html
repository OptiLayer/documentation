The concept of small-scale and large-scale root-mean-square (rms) roughness was introduced by Tikhonravov et al. in the paper “Effects of Interface Roughness on the Spectral Properties of Thin Films and Multilayers” (Applied Optics, 42, 5140-5148, 2003). It was shown that the effect of roughness on the properties of optical coatings can be adequately described by these two parameters. The terms small-scale and large-scale rms roughness refer to spatial roughness harmonics with periods small or large compared to the wavelength of the incident light. The theoretical motivation for introducing two roughness parameters is that they have different effects on the spectral characteristics of the coating. The main purpose of this example is to demonstrate this fact.

In the File -> Project menu of OptiLayer, open the directory LEC24D19.  Datafiles needed to run this exercise are already loaded into RAM. These are the substrate n=1.50, the layer materials n=1.48 and n=1.64, and the design QWM-63 at 193 nm. The effects of roughness are more pronounced in the short wavelength region, especially in the VUV spectral region. For this reason, the refractive indices of the substrate and layer materials have values typical of fluoride materials used in this region. The considered design is a 63-layer quarter-wave mirror with a central wavelength of 193nm. In the T&R&Phase window use the spectral range of 160-240 nm to view the transmittance of this design.

In the Analysis menu select the Interface roughness option. The Interface Roughness Analysis Setup window, use Characteristic tab to set the analyzed spectral characteristic and mark the type of roughness to be examined (Small scale or Large scale). Let’s start with a large scale roughness and select normal incidence transmittance as the examined characteristic. Note that this type of roughness results in light loss due to scattering.

The Roughness tab is used to set the rms levels of large scale roughness at all layer interfaces, starting with the interface of the first layer with the substrate and ending with the air boundary of the last mirror layer. We set all these values to 5 nm.

Clicking the OK button opens the Roughness Evaluation window. Adjust the wavelength range of this window to the range of 160-240 nm. There are two notable effects of the large sale roughness. This is a narrowing of the high reflection region and a significant decrease in transmittance outside this region. The last effect is, of course, due to scattering. Close the Roughness Evaluation window.

Let us turn our attention to small scale roughness. Select the Interface roughness option again. In the Characteristic tab of the Interface Roughness Analysis Setup window, select normal incidence transmittance and check Small scale roughness. Typically, small-scale roughness represents the film surface roughness associated with the deposition process. It is negligible at the substrate boundary and, in many cases, also negligible at the other layer boundaries. But there are still deposition processes and thin film materials that can create small-scale rms roughness in the range of a few nanometers. This primarily applies to fluoride films deposited by low energetic processes.

At the Roughness tab, we set the value to zero at the interface of the first layer with the substrate and set all other values to 5 nm.

Click the OK button and examine the effect of small scale roughness in the same range of 160-240 nm. The presence of small scale roughness also leads to a narrowing of the high reflection zone. But the influence of small-scale and large-scale roughness outside this zone is very different. This is due to the fact that small scale roughness does not cause light losses for scattering. But it causes some redistribution of energy between transmitted and reflected light beams. Changes in transmittance outside the high reflection zone are due to this redistribution.
